{: data-content=" Prologue "}

As I shift my attention to systems-level engineering, it feels like the right time to resume building in public. For the next five months, I’ll be permissionlessly participating in the [seventh iteration](https://github.com/eth-protocol-fellows/cohort-seven) of the Ethereum Protocol Fellowship (EPF).

I spent the past year optimising the [Ethereum Execution Specs Test framework](https://github.com/ethereum/execution-spec-tests/pulls?q=is%3Apr+author%3Arichardgreg+is%3Aclosed). While participating in the EPF study group, I was introduced to many Ethereum concepts and organisations that implement them. One concept that caught my attention was Ethereum’s goal to make the chain quantum-resistant. I attended a presentation by a member of the [Ream team](https://github.com/ReamLabs), which led me to explore the organisation, what they are building, and how my current skill set can help them achieve their goal. I discovered a project ripe for optimisation: [LeanStart](https://github.com/ReamLabs/leanstart).

----
{: data-content=" Leanstart "}

LeanStart is a devnet orchestrator for lean Ethereum clients. Since I discovered it, I have mapped out the architecture, opened a couple of client tooling and devex [PR fixes](https://github.com/ReamLabs/leanstart/pulls/richardgreg), and stress-tested the program by spinning up a 6-validator devnet (2 subnets × 3 pods = 6 Ream nodes) with a simple command: `leanstart run ream:3 --subnets 2`.

The bootstrap was flawless, but the devnet failed to sustain consensus. On my modest box (8 vCPU / 16 GB), the slot clock raced ahead to ~4,500 while the canonical chain head froze at slot 1,604 and finalisation stalled at 1,001 — roughly 90 minutes in. The nodes never crashed outright; they simply lost their peers and could never claw their way back.

<!-- Around the same time, a Ream maintainer posted logs of the same symptom in the project Telegram: a node "falling off" after running all night and finalising ~2k slots, this time on a much beefier M4 Max with 36 GB. I took a shot at diagnosing it.

The eye-catching line in those logs was `sendmsg ... os error 22; halting segmentation offload` — a tempting culprit. But it was a red herring: it's a benign, one-time networking fallback, and on one node it fired a full 3.5 minutes *after* that node had already dropped its peers. Cause can't follow effect.

The real story was quieter. A brief (~5-second) host-level freeze hit both aggregator nodes at the same instant — long enough for their QUIC connections to time out. That alone shouldn't be fatal, except the client never recovers: it re-dials every 20 seconds, every handshake times out, and it stays at zero peers for hours with finalisation frozen. The same dead-end I'd hit on my own machine, just reached through a different door.

So the proximate cause isn't a crash or the scary error — it's that a transient blip permanently partitions the network because peering can't self-heal. I still need more data to be fully conclusive (and to pin down what caused the freeze), but it's a satisfying first thread to pull. -->
